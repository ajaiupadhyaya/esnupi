# On the pleasure of pretending a website is a room

<p className="lab-lede">
  I grew up thinking that interfaces were buildings. Small ones. A keyboard was
  a brick path, a window was a window, and the hard drive was the room where
  the soul of the thing actually lived. It took me until my thirties to realize
  that no one else had ever seen it this way, and that this was, in fact, a
  very fortunate misunderstanding.
</p>

  The first file I ever saved was called `me.txt`. I have never opened it since.

## The machine is a room. The room is a bureaucracy.

A good machine has clerks in it. The clerks maintain a public face
(the chrome) and an inner life (the content). When these two are the
same person — when every surface shouts "I am the machine, also I am
the artist" — the building collapses under its own charisma. The
interface is supposed to be furniture. Furniture can have character;
furniture should not have a monologue.

<Aside>
  "Flat design" was, in hindsight, a way of making the room lose its furniture.
</Aside>

You walk into a good bureaucracy and the stamps are the stamps, the
desks are the desks, and — if you are lucky — there is something
beautiful in the middle of it all. A typewriter. A paper plane. A
window that only opens on Tuesdays.

## Two registers of voice

There is the voice of the system, which says
  CONTROL PANELS · EDIT · VIEW · SPECIAL.
And there is the voice of the body of the work, which says
  *I grew up thinking that interfaces were buildings.*
A good site lets both speak, but never at the same volume.

<Aside>
  Margin notes are where the second voice writes to the first without
  interrupting the main thought.
</Aside>
